Guardianship

Editor's Note

Purpose#

The Guardianship pattern captures the concepts and relations that explain how generic guardianship relationships work, and can be constructed. It shows that guardianships are embedded in a jurisdiction that govern such relationships.

The contribution of this pattern is to establish a building block and terminology for constructing a consistent, coherent and sufficiently complete mental model that allows parties to convey guardianship-related ideas to other parties, without running the risk of being misunderstood, by expressing any guardianship-related use-case in terms of the model. This will enable us to draft requirements and specifications for infrastructural IT, and make a start with specifying standardizable data structures (schemas) for use in combination with VCs.

Introduction#

The term 'guardianship' has many definitions/descriptions. Examples are

  • ‚ÄúThe position of protecting or defending something‚Äù or ‚ÄúThe position of being legally responsible for the care of someone who is unable to manage their own affairs.‚Äù (both from the [Oxford dictionary](https://www.lexico.com/en/definition/guardianship)),

  • ‚ÄúOne who has the care of the person or property of another‚Äù or ‚ÄúOne that guards‚Äù (both from [Merriam-Webster](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/guardianship)),

  • ‚ÄúThe state or duty of being a guardian‚Äù, where 'guardian' is defined as ‚ÄúA person who has the legal right and responsibility of taking care of someone who cannot take care of himself or herself‚Äù or ‚ÄúSomeone who protects something‚Äù (Cambridge Dictionary), or

  • ‚ÄúThe status of being a protector, advocate, or proxy for a person‚Äù ([Sovrin Guardianship Task Force whitepaper](https://sovrin.org/wp-content/uploads/Guardianship-Whitepaper.pdf)), which defines 'guardian' as ‚ÄúAn organization or person protecting another person and possibly their property‚Äù.

  • ‚ÄúThe legal, social, or organizational responsibility of serving as a Guardian‚Äù (Sovrin Glossary), which also defines 'guardian' as ‚ÄúAn Identity Owner who administers identity Data, Wallets, and/or Agents on behalf of a Dependent‚Äù.

So, it seems that most people will acknowledge that 'guardianship' is a relation between

  • someone (or an organization, collectively referred to as a 'party') or something that is 'under guardianship', i.e. being cared for, guarded, protected or defended - we call this the 'dependent', and

  • a party that does this caring, guarding, protecting or defending - we call this the 'guardian'.

Note that 'dependent' and 'guardian' are roles in a guardianship relationship; they do not have an independent existence (as e.g. a human being does). A 'dependent' (or 'guardian') only exists if someone fulfills this role in a guardianship relationship, which in turn can only exist if both the 'guardian' and 'dependent' roles are being assigned.

Example: in the Mya use-case, a guardianship relationship exists between Mya (as the dependent) and her grandmother Zo (as guardian). There is also a guardianship relationship between Mya (dependent) and UNICEF (camp staff - as guardian).

The actual activities that a guardian performs as (s)he cares for, guards, or ‚Ä¶ its dependent (in a specific guardianship relationship) differ from case to case, and from situation to situation. Still, in general we can say that a guardianship relationship comes with rights and duties that enable (or force) a guardian to execute specific actions - for the purpose of caring/guarding/‚Ä¶ its dependent.

Example: in the Mya use-case, the camp staff and Zo have the right to a daily food ration from the camp shop. Zo has the right to accompany Mya outside the camp. Etcetera.

A guardianship relationship is meaningful to the extent in which such rights and duties are actually upheld and/or enforced.

Example: if in the Mya use-case guardianship is not properly enforced, human traffickers may pose as Mya's guardian to get her out of the camp so that she can be sold as a slave.

Defining and enforcing rights and duties, as well as resolving conflicts that may arise, are the very essence of a jurisdiction - for details see the Jurisdictions pattern

The wealth in varieties in guardianship relationships can now easily be explained by observing that the various Jurisdictions all exercise their self-sovereignty as they operate their legal systems.

Example: in the Mya use-case, UNICEF sets the rules, enforces them and resolves related conflicts within its refugee camps, in its own, self-sovereign ways. This qualifies UNICEF as a jurisdiction.

For a guardianship relationship to be meaningful and relevant, it must be associated to a (single) jurisdiction that creates, modifies and dissolves the relation, specifies who the guardian and dependent are, and assigns each of them a set of rights and duties (including 'negative' rights and duties, i.e. what they may/must NOT do). A jurisdiction is implicitly tasked to enforce such rights and duties, and provide for the resolution of conflicts, yet is (and remains) self-sovereign in determining the extent in which it does so.

Example: in the Mya use-case, UNICEF camp staff Sofia (is mandated to) determine whether or not a guardianship relationship may be established between Mya and Zo, according to the rules that UNICEF has established for the camp. This kind of guardianship relationship comes with some default rights and duties for Mya and Zo. For example, the guardian gets the right to daily obtain one additional food ration from the camp store for the dependent. Also, the guardian is allowed to take the dependent for a walk outside the camp. Depending on the rules UNICEF has put in place, Sofia might not issue all default rights/duties, or issue additional ones. If Zo dies, or Mya and Zo leave the camp, this guardianship relationship becomes ineffective. In the meantime though, the rights/duties associated with the guardianship relationship may change, to be determined by (qualified) UNICEF (personel).

    • specifies Mya as the dependent in the relationship;

Building Block: Guardianship Relationship Pattern#

The figure shows that all (operational, or 'runtime') Guardianship Relationship live within the context of a specific Jurisdiction. A Guardianship Relationship links two Legal Entities (LE's) within that Jurisdiction, one which we will call the 'guardian', the other the 'Dependent'. The Guardianship Relationship specifies rights and duties to each of them.

Note that such rights and duties may refer to LE's other than the guardian or Dependent, e.g. a third party against whom a right or duty may or must be exercised. We emphasize that such references must identify these entities within the scope of the Knowledge of the party of the Jurisdiction, because however obvious this may seem, in practice people often do not realize that this must be the case for the Legal System of the Jurisdiction to work.

The Legal System of a Jurisdiction may define (named) types of Guardianship Relationships, e.g. 'Parenthood' or 'Curatorship', before any actual Guardianship Relationship exists. Legal Jurisdictions do that in their laws, while other Jurisdictions may have other, perhaps more informal ways of defining them. A Guardianship Relationship type definition will specify a set of generic rights and duties (which we call Duty/Right Types) for the entities that will be assigned the roles of guardian, dependent, and perhaps others.

It is typical of a Duty/Right Type to NOT refer to individual entities, but rather use placeholders that are to be filled in when a Duty/Right is created that is an instance of the Duty/Right Type.

So typically, a Jurisdiction will define the set of Guardianship Types that its Legal System supports. For each of them, the Jurisdiction:

  • specifies the rules/laws that govern the creation and management of their instances,

  • ptionally specifying processes (and possibly providing some or all means) to monitor/police compliance, and

  • providing a mechanism for resolving disputes related to them.

Typically, the Task of creating a Guardianship Relationship (GR) consists of:

  • associating the GR with LE's for the positions of 'guardian' and 'dependent';

  • associating the GR with an initial/default set of Hohfeldian Duties/Rights, the types of which are specified by the Guardianship Type that the GR instantiates.

  • assigning values for the placeholders that occur in the specifications of such Duties/Rights

The Task of managing a GR consists of adding, (un)suspending, revoking/removing instances of Duty/Right types that are meaningful in the Jurisdiction as appropriate, and assigning values for any placeholders therein. Also, (un)suspending and/or revoking/invalidating the GR itself is part of that Task.

Note that an artefact that contains the specification of a GR may need to take various forms. For every kind of Actor that is envisaged to read and process such a specification, it must be represented in a language (syntax and semantics) that such an Actor knows how to read and process. This could mean that the artefact is only, or both human readable (in one or more languages) or machine readable (in one or more schemas).

Notes#

Having defined a mental model for guardianship, and having applied it to the Mya use-case, we can make some generic observations.

  1. guardianships are typed in the same way that roles can be typed. The rights and duties that are associated with Roles that are assigned to e.g. a party (e.g. a department) will be quite different from those that are associated with Roles that are assigned to employees, or customers. We see this also happening with guardianships: the rights and duties that are associated with a guardianship relationship where the guardian is the party that operates the legal system of a Jurisdiction and the dependent is a refugee is quite different from a guardianship relationship where the guardian and dependent are both refugees. One such difference is that in the first case, certain rights and duties can be delegated whereas in the latter case that is prohibited.

  2. the meaning of guardianship relationships is defined by a Jurisdiction in the same way as the meaning of roles are defined by a Jurisdiction. This meaning is given by the set of rights and duties that are assigned to the role/guardianship, which in turn depend on the objectives that the Jurisdiction aims to contribute to by assigning them, and the Transaction Types and Transaction Type Roles it specifies in relation to this.

  3. perhaps guardianship is not all that special: at a functional level, they work quite the same as roles do, the difference only lying in the fact that roles are an assignment of rights, duties etc. to a (semantically properly defined) single legal entity (e.g. a Refugee, an Employee, etc.), whereas a guardianship is an assignment of rights, duties etc. to a (semantically properly defined) ordered pair of Legal Entities (the first of which is called 'guardian' and the other is called 'dependent').

  4. in a way, ownership may be considered a specialization of guardianship. This is supported by the various definitions we find in different dictionaries - which all have a striking resemblance with definitions of 'guardianship'. Also, it is supported by the fact that:

  • a party that owns something (an entity) is entitled to do transactions (with that entity) that other Parties are not entitled to, e.g. selling it.

  • a party that owns an entity typically protects or defends such entities (their property); the party may also take care of it.

  • like guardianships, duties, rights, privileges etc. are being assigned to ownership by a Jurisdiction.

  • in the same way that a Jurisdiction may have different kinds of guardianship, they may have different kinds of ownership - a property that is well-known e.g. for corporations - and each kind of ownership may serve its own purposes.

One might argue that because of these similarities, 'ownership' and 'guardianship' are the same. However, there is a difference, which is can be found in the kind of objectives that (the party that governs) the jurisdiction) pursues:

  • ownership is about recognizing one's right to enjoy and (given some limitations) absolutely control something;
  • guardianship is more about a duty to care, defend and protect someone or something.

Since the Jurisdiction model claims that Jurisdictions own Objectives, Guardianship Types, and other things, this means that there is recursion, implying that Jurisdictions are guardians of these things they own. Indeed, Jurisdictions do have (implicit or explicit) rules that govern their Objectives, Guardianship Types, and whatever else they may have (by the very definition of Jurisdiction).
